¿What do we do?

OceanGuard is dedicated to protecting and restoring the world's oceans

Our Vision

OceanGuard aims to achieve political changes in the countries responsible for most of the oceans to increase biodiversity and the abundance of marine life.

OceanGuard, founded in 2001, is the largest international organization focused exclusively on ocean conservation. Our delegations around the world work together to win strategic campaigns, aimed at achieving tangible results that help us restore the health and biodiversity of the oceans.

We envision a future where the world's oceans teem with life. Where huge schools of anchovies, sardines and other fish are commonplace. Where marlins, sharks and tuna roam the seas in great numbers; where coral gardens, seagrass meadows and other seascapes thrive and sustain life; where dolphins, whales and sea turtles flourish; where local fishing economies and cultures flourish rather than decline; and where shellfish is an abundant and healthy source of food for hundreds of millions of people.

The oceans cover 71% of the planet and are essential for life on Earth. They are the place where most living things gather and play a central role in the world's natural systems, for example in regulating the climate and absorbing carbon dioxide. They represent the livelihood of millions of fishermen and others around the world. The seas also feed hundreds of millions of people.

Unfortunately, they are in danger: scientists tell us that the amount of fish caught in the oceans has begun to decline, for the first time in history, in decades. Fortunately, we know how to fix things. Where fisheries have been managed with catch limits based on scientific advice, reducing by-catch and protecting habitats, the oceans are beginning to recover. OceanGuard is dedicated to promoting science-based fisheries management and the recovery of the world's oceans.

The Problem

The oceans are vast, but they are not immune to human influence. We have already altered or destroyed many marine ecosystems and have brought species millions of years old to the brink of extinction. According to a study published in Science, less than 4% of the oceans remain unaffected by human activity.
